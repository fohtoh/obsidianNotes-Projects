# Common Chord Progressions in Popular Music

## Introduction

Chord progressions are the backbone of most songs. They're described using Roman numerals or scale degrees (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7) that represent chords built on each note of the scale. This system allows the same progression to work in any key.

**Key:**
- Uppercase Roman numerals (I, IV, V) = Major chords
- Lowercase Roman numerals (ii, iii, vi) = Minor chords
- Numbers (1, 4, 5, 6) can represent the scale degree or chord root

---

## The Most Common Progressions

### 1. **I - V - vi - IV** (1-5-6-4)

**Also known as:** The "Pop-Punk Progression" or "Sensitive Female Chord Progression"

**Example in C Major:** C - G - Am - F

**Why it works:** Creates a perfect balance of resolution (I-V) and emotion (vi), ending with a strong subdominant (IV) that wants to resolve back to I.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Let It Be" - The Beatles
- "Don't Stop Believin'" - Journey
- "Someone Like You" - Adele
- "With or Without You" - U2
- "No Woman No Cry" - Bob Marley
- "Poker Face" - Lady Gaga
- "Torn" - Natalie Imbruglia
- "Payphone" - Maroon 5
- "Titanium" - David Guetta ft. Sia

---

### 2. **I - IV - V** (1-4-5)

**Also known as:** The "Three-Chord Song" or "Rock 'n' Roll Progression"

**Example in C Major:** C - F - G

**Why it works:** Uses the three primary chords of the key. Simple, powerful, and the foundation of rock, blues, and country music.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Twist and Shout" - The Beatles
- "Wild Thing" - The Troggs
- "La Bamba" - Ritchie Valens
- "Louie Louie" - The Kingsmen
- "Good Riddance (Time of Your Life)" - Green Day
- "Blitzkrieg Bop" - Ramones
- "What I Got" - Sublime
- "Sweet Home Alabama" - Lynyrd Skynyrd (I-IV-I-V pattern)

---

### 3. **vi - IV - I - V** (6-4-1-5)

**Also known as:** The "Canon Progression" (from Pachelbel's Canon)

**Example in C Major:** Am - F - C - G

**Why it works:** Starts with the relative minor creating emotional depth, then resolves through familiar territory.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Basket Case" - Green Day
- "Complicated" - Avril Lavigne
- "Grenade" - Bruno Mars
- "Numb" - Linkin Park
- "All the Small Things" - Blink-182
- "Kids" - MGMT
- "Hook" - Blues Traveler
- "Graduation (Friends Forever)" - Vitamin C

---

### 4. **I - V - IV** (1-5-4)

**Also known as:** The "Garage Rock Progression"

**Example in C Major:** C - G - F

**Why it works:** Similar to I-IV-V but the IV comes after the V, creating a different resolution feeling. Very common in folk and rock.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Knockin' on Heaven's Door" - Bob Dylan
- "Free Fallin'" - Tom Petty
- "Torn" - Natalie Imbruglia (verses)
- "What's Up?" - 4 Non Blondes
- "You're Gonna Go Far, Kid" - The Offspring
- "Glycerine" - Bush

---

### 5. **I - vi - IV - V** (1-6-4-5)

**Also known as:** The "50s Progression" or "Doo-Wop Progression"

**Example in C Major:** C - Am - F - G

**Why it works:** Classic progression that cycles smoothly through chords, common in oldies and ballads.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Stand By Me" - Ben E. King
- "Every Breath You Take" - The Police
- "Unchained Melody" - The Righteous Brothers
- "Blue Moon" - The Marcels
- "Crocodile Rock" - Elton John
- "The Lion Sleeps Tonight" - The Tokens
- "Friday I'm In Love" - The Cure

---

### 6. **I - IV - vi - V** (1-4-6-5)

**Example in C Major:** C - F - Am - G

**Why it works:** Similar to the I-V-vi-IV but reordered for a different emotional flow.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Beast of Burden" - The Rolling Stones
- "She Will Be Loved" - Maroon 5
- "Jessie's Girl" - Rick Springfield
- "Africa" - Toto (slight variation)

---

### 7. **ii - V - I** (2-5-1)

**Also known as:** The "Jazz Turnaround" or "Jazz Cadence"

**Example in C Major:** Dm - G - C

**Why it works:** Creates strong harmonic movement and resolution. Fundamental in jazz but also used in pop and R&B.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Sunday Morning" - Maroon 5
- "Autumn Leaves" - Jazz Standard
- "Fly Me to the Moon" - Frank Sinatra
- "Satin Doll" - Duke Ellington
- "Still D.R.E." - Dr. Dre (uses ii-V)

---

### 8. **I - iii - IV - V** (1-3-4-5)

**Example in C Major:** C - Em - F - G

**Why it works:** The iii chord adds color between I and IV, creating a smooth stepwise bass movement.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Riptide" - Vance Joy (uses variation)
- "Blue Moon" - variations use this
- "Heart and Soul" - Hoagy Carmichael

---

### 9. **I - bVII - IV** (1-b7-4)

**Also known as:** The "Mixolydian Vamp" or "Modal Progression"

**Example in C Major:** C - Bb - F

**Why it works:** Borrowing the bVII from the Mixolydian mode creates a rock sound that avoids the traditional V chord.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Sweet Child O' Mine" - Guns N' Roses
- "Dreams" - Fleetwood Mac
- "Sympathy for the Devil" - The Rolling Stones
- "Hey Jude" - The Beatles (in verses)
- "Seven Nation Army" - The White Stripes

---

### 10. **i - bVI - bIII - bVII** (1-b6-b3-b7)

**Also known as:** The "Natural Minor Descending Progression"

**Example in A minor:** Am - F - C - G

**Why it works:** Uses all natural minor chords with a descending bass line feel. Creates a dark, driving energy perfect for rock, punk, and alternative music. The progression avoids the traditional dominant (V) chord, giving it a modal, less resolved quality.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Self Esteem" - The Offspring
- "Zombie" - The Cranberries
- "Numb" - Linkin Park (similar)
- "Running Up That Hill" - Kate Bush (variation)

---

### 11. **i - bVI - bVII** (1-b6-b7)

**Also known as:** The "Aeolian Progression" (in minor)

**Example in A minor:** Am - F - G

**Why it works:** Three-chord natural minor progression that sounds dark and powerful. Common in rock and metal. This is essentially the shorter version of the i-bVI-bIII-bVII progression.

**Famous Songs:**
- "All Along the Watchtower" - Jimi Hendrix
- "Stairway to Heaven" - Led Zeppelin (verses)
- "Smells Like Teen Spirit" - Nirvana (variation)

---

### 12. **I - iii - vi - IV** (1-3-6-4)

**Example in C Major:** C - Em - Am - F

**Why it works:** Smooth descending thirds in the bass create a flowing, melodic progression.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Take Me to Church" - Hozier (variation)
- "Love Yourself" - Justin Bieber
- "Counting Stars" - OneRepublic

---

### 13. **12-Bar Blues**

**Pattern:** I - I - I - I - IV - IV - I - I - V - IV - I - V

**Example in C Major:**
Bars 1-4: C | C | C | C |
Bars 5-8: F | F | C | C |
Bars 9-12: G | F | C | G (turnaround)

**Why it works:** The foundation of blues, rock, and early rock 'n' roll.

**Famous Songs:**
- "Johnny B. Goode" - Chuck Berry
- "Hound Dog" - Elvis Presley
- "Pride and Joy" - Stevie Ray Vaughan
- "Crossroads" - Cream
- "The Thrill Is Gone" - B.B. King

## How to Use This Guide

### 1. **Transpose to Any Key**
These progressions work in any key. If a song is in G Major:
- I = G Major
- IV = C Major
- V = D Major
- vi = Em

### 2. **Experiment with Variations**
- Change the order of chords
- Add 7ths, 9ths, or sus chords for color
- Try the same progression in a different rhythm

### 3. **Analyze Songs You Love**
- Figure out which progression they use
- Notice how the melody interacts with the chords
- Many songs use multiple progressions for verse, chorus, and bridge

### 4. **Write Your Own Songs**
- Start with a progression you like
- Add your own melody and lyrics
- Change one chord to make it unique

### 5. **Practice on Your Instrument**
- Play these progressions in multiple keys
- Sing or improvise melodies over them
- Develop muscle memory for smooth transitions

## Tips for Understanding Progressions

### Roman Numeral System
- **I, IV, V** = Major chords (strong, bright)
- **ii, iii, vi** = Minor chords (softer, darker)
- **viiÂ°** = Diminished chord (tense, needs resolution)

### Functional Harmony
- **I (Tonic)** = Home base, resolution
- **IV (Subdominant)** = Movement away from home
- **V (Dominant)** = Tension that wants to resolve to I
- **vi (Relative minor)** = Emotional alternative to I

### Common Substitutions
- **vi can substitute for I** (both are tonic function)
- **ii can substitute for IV** (both are subdominant function)
- **iii can substitute for V or I** (weaker but adds color)

## Why These Progressions Are So Common

1. **They sound good to our ears** - Western music has conditioned us to find these pleasing
2. **They're easy to play** - Most use simple, common chords
3. **They're memorable** - The patterns create expectation and satisfaction
4. **They're versatile** - Work across genres from pop to rock to country
5. **They work with melodies** - Provide a strong harmonic foundation for singing
